This guidance helps facilitators and members of service communities develop their own by showing how others have done it before. You can help us keep this guidance up to date by sharing the outcomes you have achieved in your own service community.

Service communities in government is a cross-government blog where facilitators and members of service communities share experiences and ideas.

Before you start a service community

  • Check which service communities exist already to ensure that you are not solving the same problem as someone else
  • Email the service communities team if you are thinking of starting a service community. They can help you get the scope right and find people in your service area
  • Think about who needs to be involved. Include people from policy, operations and digital, and anyone else that helps to shape the service across all level of seniority
  • Articulate why you need a service community. Your proposal should include the problems a community will focus on and the departments likely to be involved

How to create a proposal document

Understand and agree on the scope

  • Create a service landscape. This is a map of all services in the end-to-end service across channels and organisations. It will help you get a common understanding of the problem space and the scope of your community
  • Agree ways of working and objectives in your community
  • Set up communication channels, so that members of the service community can keep in contact. Make sure these channels are inclusive and accessible to everyone

Case studies that can help you scope your service community:
How service landscapes help service communities, The benefit of clear objectives for communities, Tips for setting up communication channels

Work together to develop your service community

  • Agree a common language in your community, so that you are all referring to the same thing when talking about your service
  • Create a step-by-step to help simplify the user jouney on GOV.UK
  • Get an understanding of what everyone is working on now and in the future, so that it is easier to plan work across departments
  • Create a community backlog based on your community objectives, so that you can track your progress
  • Create a set of community personas so that the community agrees on who their users are
  • Share resources including research and performance data, so that you can understand how the end-to-end service is used
